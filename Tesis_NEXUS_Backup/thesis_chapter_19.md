# Chapter 19: Clinical Case Studies in CCA and SPIA

## 19.1 Introduction: Learning from the Bedside

Theoretical frameworks and statistical models gain their ultimate validity through clinical application. This chapter presents a series of anonymized clinical case studies illustrating the spectrum of Signal-Induced Algorithmic Pareidolia (SPIA) and Cyber-Acoustic Convergence (CCA) presentations, diagnostic processes, and treatment outcomes. Each case highlights key learning points for clinicians encountering similar presentations.

## 19.2 Case 1: The Night-Shift Call Center Worker

### 19.2.1 Presenting Complaint

**Patient**: Maria, 28-year-old female
**Referred by**: Employee Assistance Program
**Chief Complaint**: "I'm hearing whispered threats during customer calls"

### 19.2.2 History of Present Illness

Maria works night shifts at a telecommunications customer service center. Over the past three months, she has increasingly heard "whispering voices" during and immediately after customer calls. The voices say brief phrases like "we're watching" or "you're next." Episodes occur 5-10 times per shift, lasting 2-3 seconds each.

She denies any voice experiences outside of work or when not using the work headset. She has no psychiatric history, denies substance use, and reports good sleep despite night shift work. She has become anxious about going to work and is considering quitting.

### 19.2.3 Assessment

**RCTT Score**: 5/5 (High CCA probability)
- ✅ Always associated with electronic device (work headset)
- ✅ Stops when not on calls
- ✅ Reports frequent static and "robotic" customer voices
- ✅ Started after company switched to new VoIP system 4 months ago
- ✅ Attempted to record with phone; captured garbled audio

**CCA-CIP Score**: 14 (High CCA probability)

**SPIA-SS Score**: 42/60 (High susceptibility)
- Pattern-seeking subscale: 18 (high)
- Tech exposure subscale: 16 (high—8+ hours daily headset use)
- Contextual risk subscale: 8 (moderate—night shift fatigue)

**Psychiatric Evaluation**: PANSS negative for psychotic symptoms. PHQ-9: 8 (mild depression, reactive). GAD-7: 14 (moderate anxiety).

**Forensic Audio Analysis**: Review of call recordings from Maria's shifts showed:
- VoIP codec using ultra-low bitrate (8 kbps G.729)
- Frequent packet loss events creating rhythmic clicking (PC1 score: 0.82)
- Spectral artifacts in 300-600 Hz range (PC2 score: 0.71)
- PCA classification: "High SPIA Potential"

### 19.2.4 Diagnosis

**Primary**: Signal-Induced Algorithmic Pareidolia (SPIA), occupational exposure
**Secondary**: Adjustment Disorder with Anxiety, related to SPIA-induced distress

### 19.2.5 Treatment

**Tier 1 (Signal Remediation)**:
- Discussed findings with employer's IT department.
- Upgraded VoIP codec to wideband (G.722) for Maria's station.
- Provided high-quality noise-canceling headset.

**Tier 2 (Psychoeducation)**:
- Two sessions explaining SPIA mechanism.
- Shared spectral analysis showing artifact patterns.
- Normalized experience: "Your brain is doing what it's designed to do."

**Tier 3 (Anxiety Management)**:
- Brief CBT (4 sessions) for work-related anxiety.
- Grounding techniques for in-call use.

### 19.2.6 Outcome

- SPIA episodes: 5-10/shift → 0-1/shift within 2 weeks of codec upgrade.
- Anxiety symptoms: GAD-7 14 → 5 at 6-week follow-up.
- Returned to full work function; no longer considering resignation.

### 19.2.7 Key Learning Points

1. **Occupational SPIA** can present as apparent psychosis but has technological etiology.
2. **IT collaboration** is essential for Tier 1 intervention.
3. **Codec quality** is a modifiable workplace factor with significant mental health impact.
4. **Rapid response** to signal remediation confirms diagnosis.

## 19.3 Case 2: The Elderly Widower

### 19.3.1 Presenting Complaint

**Patient**: Harold, 76-year-old male
**Referred by**: Primary care physician
**Chief Complaint**: "My late wife speaks to me through the radio"

### 19.3.2 History of Present Illness

Harold's wife died 18 months ago. For the past 6 months, he has heard her voice coming from his bedroom radio, especially at night. The voice is "unclear but definitely her," saying comforting phrases like "I miss you" and "I'm okay." He finds the experience "strange but not frightening" and has not told his family for fear of being "put in a home."

He has mild hearing loss (uses hearing aids) and mild cognitive impairment (MMSE 24/30). He denies depression—his grief has been manageable. The radio is an old AM model, used primarily for talk shows.

### 19.3.3 Assessment

**RCTT Score**: 3/5 (Moderate CCA probability)
- ✅ Associated with radio (electronic device)
- ⚠️ Doesn't completely stop when radio is off (some continuation in silence immediately after)
- ✅ Poor audio quality on old AM radio
- ❌ No recent change—same radio for years
- ❌ Has not recorded

**CCA-CIP Score**: 9 (Moderate CCA probability)

**Differential Considerations**:
- CCA/SPIA (AM radio artifacts)
- Musical Ear Syndrome (hearing loss + pattern-seeking)
- Grief hallucinosis (bereaved individuals commonly hear deceased)
- Early dementia with psychotic features

**Audiological Evaluation**: Moderate bilateral sensorineural hearing loss, well-fitted hearing aids.

**Radio Analysis**: Recording of the radio's audio revealed:
- Significant AM static and fading
- Intermittent crosstalk from adjacent frequencies
- Spectral content in voice-frequency range but highly distorted

### 19.3.4 Diagnosis

**Primary**: Signal-Induced Algorithmic Pareidolia (SPIA), superimposed on grief-related perceptual priming
**Contributing**: Hearing loss (increased pattern-seeking to compensate)
**Rule-Out**: Musical Ear Syndrome (ongoing monitoring)

### 19.3.5 Treatment

**Tier 1 (Signal Remediation)**:
- Replaced AM radio with digital FM radio (cleaner signal).
- Encouraged use of hearing aids while listening to radio.

**Tier 2 (Psychoeducation)**:
- Gentle explanation of SPIA without dismissing his grief experience.
- Framed as: "Your love for your wife makes your brain especially good at hearing her patterns in noise."

**Family Involvement**:
- Educated daughter about SPIA to reduce fear of dementia.
- Reassured that this is not a sign of "going crazy."

**Monitoring**:
- Cognitive reassessment at 6 months (stable at MMSE 24).
- Watch for progression to Musical Ear Syndrome or new psychopathology.

### 19.3.6 Outcome

- SPIA episodes: Reduced from nightly to occasional (1-2x/week) with new radio.
- Harold reports experience is "less clear now, almost gone" with digital radio.
- Initially ambivalent ("I kind of liked hearing her") but accepted explanation.
- No distress; continues to function independently.

### 19.3.7 Key Learning Points

1. **Elderly patients** may experience SPIA with benign content (comfort rather than threat).
2. **Grief priming** combines with SPIA to produce personalized voice content.
3. **Hearing loss** increases vulnerability—evaluate audiology in all cases.
4. **Respect the meaning** of the experience even while explaining the mechanism.

## 19.4 Case 3: The Paranoid Professional

### 19.4.1 Presenting Complaint

**Patient**: David, 42-year-old male, software engineer
**Self-referred**: Via telemedicine
**Chief Complaint**: "My phone is transmitting coded messages about me to unknown parties"

### 19.4.2 History of Present Illness

David is convinced that his smartphone emits ultrasonic signals containing encoded information about his activities. He "hears" fragments of his own words and computer screen content repeated back at him through the phone speaker when it's idle. He has purchased RF detection equipment and has recorded hundreds of hours of "evidence."

He has isolated himself from colleagues, believing they are "in on it." He has taken his phone apart looking for "extra chips." He does not sleep well and has lost 15 pounds in 3 months.

No prior psychiatric history; family history positive for a brother with schizophrenia.

### 19.4.3 Assessment

**RCTT Score**: 4/5 (High CCA surface probability)
- ⚠️ BUT: The interpretive framework is delusional
- His "evidence" of surveillance is SPIA of his own ambient speech
- He attributes it to persecution rather than technology glitch

**PANSS**: Positive symptoms score elevated (P1 delusions: 5, P6 suspiciousness: 6)

**Forensic Audio Analysis** (of his "evidence recordings"):
- Recording reveals typical smartphone microphone self-noise and ambient compression artifacts
- No ultrasonic content detected
- Spectral analysis shows SPIA-potential artifacts, but no encoded data

**Clinical Impression**: This is **NOT pure CCA/SPIA**. David is experiencing genuine SPIA events, but his *interpretation* of these events is delusional. He has incorporated technologically real artifacts into a paranoid belief system.

### 19.4.4 Diagnosis

**Primary**: Schizophrenia, first episode (or Delusional Disorder, persecutory type—needs observation)
**Secondary/Contributing**: Signal-Induced Algorithmic Pareidolia (SPIA) as "evidence substrate" for delusions

### 19.4.5 Treatment

**Immediate**:
- Antipsychotic medication initiated (aripiprazole 10 mg → 15 mg).
- Psychiatric hospitalization declined; agreed to intensive outpatient.

**Addressing SPIA Component**:
- Forensic audio analysis results shared with David after 2 weeks of medication. 
- At that point (reduced delusional intensity), he was able to consider alternative explanation.
- Psychoeducation about SPIA framed as: "Your brain did detect real artifacts, but the technology explanation is more likely than surveillance."

**Tier 1 (Signal Remediation)**:
- Encouraged replacing phone (symbolic break from "contaminated" device).
- APF software installed on new device.

**Long-term**:
- Continued antipsychotic medication.
- CBT for residual paranoia.
- Career counseling (returned to work part-time at 4 months).

### 19.4.6 Outcome

- Delusional beliefs: Largely remitted on medication (P1: 5 → 2 at 8 weeks).
- SPIA experiences: Eliminated with new phone and APF.
- Insight: Partial—"I still think something was weird, but I accept it's probably not surveillance."
- Function: Returned to work with accommodations; maintains family relationships.

### 19.4.7 Key Learning Points

1. **SPIA can coexist with psychosis**—it becomes substrate for delusional elaboration.
2. **Do not assume all technology-associated voices are CCA**—evaluate the interpretive framework.
3. **Family history of schizophrenia** is a major risk factor requiring careful psychiatric evaluation.
4. **Staged approach**: Treat psychosis first, then address SPIA component.
5. **Forensic audio analysis** can be therapeutic once delusions are reduced—provides reality anchor.

## 19.5 Case 4: The Anxious Teenager

### 19.5.1 Presenting Complaint

**Patient**: Emma, 16-year-old female
**Referred by**: School counselor
**Chief Complaint**: "I hear my name being called when I'm doing homework online"

### 19.5.2 History of Present Illness

Emma reports hearing her name called in a "creepy whisper" approximately 2-3 times per week over the past 2 months. It happens only when she is doing homework on her laptop in her room, usually late at night with headphones on. She uses low-quality Bluetooth earbuds and streams lo-fi hip-hop study music while working.

She is otherwise high-functioning: honor roll student, active in tennis, healthy friendships. Parents are concerned about "early schizophrenia" given media portrayals.

### 19.5.3 Assessment

**RCTT Score**: 4/5 (High CCA probability)

**SPIA-SS Score**: 38/60 (Moderate-high susceptibility)
- High pattern-seeking (creative, imaginative teenager)
- High tech exposure (6+ hours daily screen time)
- Contextual: Often tired when it happens (late night studying)

**Audio Analysis**:
- Reviewed the lo-fi study music playlists Emma uses.
- Several tracks contain sampled, heavily processed vocals (intentionally lo-fidelity aesthetic).
- Low bitrate Bluetooth codec compressing already-degraded audio.
- Combined spectral analysis: Extremely high SPIA potential.

**Psychiatric Evaluation**: No psychotic symptoms. Mild test anxiety (GAD-7: 10). No depression (PHQ-A: 4).

### 19.5.4 Diagnosis

**Primary**: Signal-Induced Algorithmic Pareidolia (SPIA), recreational/study audio exposure
**Secondary**: Test anxiety (mild, not requiring treatment)

### 19.5.5 Treatment

**Tier 1 (Signal Remediation)**:
- Replaced Bluetooth earbuds with quality wired headphones.
- Changed study music from lo-fi hip-hop to instrumental classical (less SPIA potential).

**Tier 2 (Psychoeducation)**:
- Age-appropriate explanation of SPIA.
- Demonstration: Played back specific lo-fi tracks and showed where "voices" emerge.
- "Your brain is just too good at finding patterns in music!"

**Family Reassurance**:
- Extensive discussion with parents about why this is NOT schizophrenia.
- Provided literature on differential diagnosis.

### 19.5.6 Outcome

- SPIA episodes: Eliminated within 1 week of audio changes.
- Emma finds the explanation "pretty cool" and has shared it with friends.
- Parents relieved; no further concerns.
- Follow-up at 3 months: No recurrence, doing well academically and socially.

### 19.5.7 Key Learning Points

1. **Teenagers are high SPIA risk** due to chronic low-quality audio exposure.
2. **Lo-fi music genre** is intentionally artifact-heavy—investigate audio content.
3. **Parental catastrophizing** is common—education is as important for family as patient.
4. **Simple interventions** (headphone upgrade, music change) can eliminate symptoms.
5. **Teenager engagement**: Framing SPIA as "your brain being cool" aids acceptance.

## 19.6 Case 5: The Rural Patient

### 19.6.1 Brief Summary

**Patient**: Juan, 52-year-old male, agricultural worker, rural Mexico (via teleneuropsychiatry)
**Complaint**: "Voices from the phone lines accusing me of sins"
**Context**: Limited cellular infrastructure, 2G GSM network, frequently calls family

**Assessment**: RCTT 5/5. GSM compression at lowest bitrate. Cultural/religious interpretation of SPIA as demonic.

**Treatment Challenges**:
- Cannot upgrade infrastructure.
- Limited access to mental health services.
- Strong religious belief system.

**Approach**:
- Involved local pastor (culturally appropriate collaborator).
- Psychoeducation reframed: "The phone's electronics make confusing sounds; this is a technology test, not a spiritual attack."
- Recommended using text messages for important communication.
- Continued pastoral support for spiritual distress.

**Outcome**: Moderate improvement; reduced distress, continued intermittent SPIA with acceptance.

### 19.6.2 Key Learning Points

1. **CCA is a global phenomenon** with infrastructure disparities.
2. **Cultural framing** must be respected and incorporated.
3. **Alternative communication** (text) can be a workaround.
4. **Community collaborators** (clergy, community health workers) extend reach.

## 19.8 Conclusion: The Clinical Art of CCA Detection

These cases illustrate the range of CCA presentations—from straightforward occupational SPIA to complex hybrid cases with underlying psychosis. Key clinical skills include:

1. **Maintaining diagnostic humility**: Neither over-diagnosing SPIA (missing psychosis) nor ignoring it (missing technology contribution).
2. **Forensic audio analysis competence**: Ability to interpret spectral data or collaborate with engineers.
3. **Cultural sensitivity**: Non-judgmental engagement with spiritual/religious interpretations.
4. **Flexibility**: Adapting interventions to available resources and patient context.

Each voice-hearing patient deserves the question: "What were you listening to?"
