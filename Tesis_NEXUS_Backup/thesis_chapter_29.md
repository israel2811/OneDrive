# Chapter 29: Social Dimension — Stalkerware, Harassment, and Harm

## 29.1 Introduction: Beyond the Technical

CCA and SPIA do not exist in a vacuum. They occur in a social context where surveillance is not merely theoretical—it is a documented reality affecting millions. This chapter examines the social dimension: the intersection of technology, harassment, abuse, and mental health that forms the backdrop for many CCA cases.

## 29.2 The Scale of the Problem

### 29.2.1 Stalkerware Statistics

**Global Data (Coalition Against Stalkerware + Industry Reports)**:
| Metric | Figure | Source Year |
|--------|--------|-------------|
| Annual detections (coalition members) | ~50,000/month | 2023 |
| 3-year growth rate | +239% | 2020-2023 |
| Devices affected (estimated) | 500,000+ annually | 2023 |
| Primary context | Intimate partner violence | - |
| Gender of victims (IPV context) | ~85% female | - |

### 29.2.2 Underreporting

These figures represent detected, reported cases. Actual prevalence is likely higher due to:
- Victims unaware of installation
- Detection tools not installed
- Fear of removing surveillance (retaliation risk)
- Normalization of monitoring in some relationships

### 29.2.3 Beyond Stalkerware

Surveillance extends beyond dedicated apps:
- Misuse of "Find My" and family locator apps
- Coerced account sharing
- Employer monitoring (often legal, sometimes abusive)
- State surveillance (varies by jurisdiction)

## 29.3 The Harassment-Technology Nexus

### 29.3.1 Technology-Facilitated Abuse

Modern harassment frequently involves technology:
| Form | Technology Vector | Psychological Impact |
|------|-------------------|---------------------|
| Location tracking | GPS, Find My, stalkerware | Loss of safe spaces |
| Communication monitoring | Message apps, email | Self-censorship, isolation |
| Audio surveillance | Microphone access | Inability to speak freely |
| Image-based abuse | Camera access, stored photos | Shame, coercion |
| Account compromise | Password theft, social engineering | Identity disruption |

### 29.3.2 Coercive Control and Technology

In IPV contexts, technology surveillance is part of coercive control:
- Monitor all communications
- Track all movements
- Control access to devices
- Use information for intimidation
- Create perception of omniscience

**This context is directly relevant to CCA**: Some patients with SPIA symptoms may have genuine reasons to believe they are monitored, based on abusive relationship history.

### 29.3.3 The Credibility Problem

Victims of technology-facilitated abuse often face:
- Disbelief ("You're paranoid")
- Gaslighting ("I would never do that")
- Lack of technical verification capacity
- Police/courts unfamiliar with stalkerware

**When dismissed, victims may develop SPIA** as hypervigilance combines with acoustic ambiguity.

## 29.4 Mental Health Impacts

### 29.4.1 Documented Effects of Surveillance

Research on technology-facilitated abuse shows:
| Impact | Prevalence | Mechanism |
|--------|------------|-----------|
| Anxiety | High | Constant uncertainty |
| Depression | High | Learned helplessness |
| PTSD symptoms | Moderate-High | Ongoing threat |
| Hypervigilance | Very High | Adaptive response |
| Sleep disturbance | High | Cannot feel safe |
| Social isolation | High | Self-protective withdrawal |

### 29.4.2 The Hypervigilance-SPIA Pathway

```
Actual surveillance → Awareness of monitoring
         │
         ▼
Hypervigilance ← ← ← ← ← Confirmed suspicion
         │                      │
         ▼                      │
Pattern-seeking ← ← ← ← ← ← ← ←┘
         │
         ▼
Ambiguous audio exposure (phone calls, ambient noise)
         │
         ▼
SPIA: Perceive speech patterns in noise
         │
         ▼
Contents match surveillance concerns
         │
         ▼
Conviction reinforced (confirmation bias)
```

**Key insight**: Real surveillance can cause SPIA even after surveillance has ended.

### 29.4.3 Differential Diagnosis Challenge

Clinicians face a dilemma:
- Dismissing surveillance claims may be dangerous (real threat unaddressed)
- Validating all claims may reinforce unfounded beliefs
- Standard psychiatric framing (delusion) may re-traumatize IPV survivors

**CCA assessment provides a pathway**: Neither dismiss nor confirm, but assess systematically (P0-P4).

## 29.5 Cyberbullying and Adolescent SPIA

### 29.5.1 Youth-Specific Vulnerabilities

Adolescents face unique risks:
- Developmental neuroplasticity → higher susceptibility
- Social media always on → constant exposure
- Cyberbullying commonplace → normalized harassment
- Less developed coping mechanisms → greater impact

### 29.5.2 Statistics on Youth Harassment

| Metric | Figure |
|--------|--------|
| Teens experiencing cyberbullying | ~37% (US) |
| Associated with suicidal ideation | 2-3x increase |
| Associated with depression | Significant correlation |
| Associated with voice-hearing | Under-researched |

### 29.5.3 SPIA in Adolescent Context

Adolescents reporting voice-hearing during phone/social media use:
- May reflect SPIA from degraded audio
- May reflect anxiety-driven hypervigilance
- May reflect genuine harassment (voice calls, voice messages)
- Requires careful differentiation

## 29.6 Suicide Risk and Harassment

### 29.6.1 The Pathway

Technology-facilitated abuse → Mental health deterioration → Suicidal ideation/behavior

**This is documented reality, not theoretical.**

### 29.6.2 Mediating Factors

| Factor | Role |
|--------|------|
| Social support | Protective if present |
| Escape options | Protective if available |
| Previous mental health | Vulnerability multiplier |
| Duration of abuse | Cumulative harm |
| Age of victim | Youth at higher risk |

### 29.6.3 Implications for CCA Assessment

When assessing SPIA in harassment contexts:
- Always assess suicide risk
- Provide appropriate safety resources
- Do not delay intervention for technical assessment
- Balance investigation with immediate support

## 29.7 Policy and Public Health Implications

### 29.7.1 Current Policy Gaps

## 29.8 Ethical Research in This Domain

### 29.8.1 Risks of Research

Studying stalkerware and SPIA risks:
- Providing "how-to" information for abusers
- Re-traumatizing research participants
- Generating findings that dismiss legitimate concerns
- Generating findings that validate unfounded claims

### 29.8.2 Safeguards

This research incorporates:
- **Defensive framing**: No operational attack guidance
- **Trauma-informed methods**: Prioritize participant wellbeing
- **Balanced interpretation**: Neither dismiss nor validate uncritically
- **Stakeholder involvement**: Input from survivor advocacy groups
- **Ethics review**: Full institutional oversight

### 29.8.3 Communication Responsibility

Findings must be communicated to:
- Avoid minimizing real surveillance threats
- Avoid amplifying conspiracy thinking
- Inform clinical practice constructively
- Support evidence-based policy

## 29.9 Intersectionality

### 29.9.1 Who Is Most Affected

Technology-facilitated abuse is not equally distributed:
| Factor | Effect |
|--------|--------|
| Gender | Women disproportionately targeted |

### 29.9.2 Cultural Considerations

SPIA assessment must account for:

- Religious attribution of voice-hearing
- Language access to resources

## 29.10 Case Integration: IPV Survivor with SPIA

**History**: 34-year-old woman, recently left abusive relationship. Partner confirmed to have installed stalkerware (detected and removed).

**Current report**: Hears partner's voice during phone calls, even when calling new contacts. Voices are threatening. Reports high anxiety.

**Assessment**:
- **P0**: SPIA symptoms; significant distress; history of confirmed surveillance
- **P1**: Device audited; stalkerware removed; no current indicators
- **P2**: CNG artifacts detected during calls; high SPIA potential
- **P3**: Voice perception reduces when using landline (no VAD/CNG)

**Diagnosis**: Scenario B (SPIA) with etiological context of prior Scenario A (actual surveillance)

**Treatment**:
- Tier 1: Signal remediation (higher quality calls)
- Tier 2: Psychoeducation (SPIA mechanism)
- Tier 3: Trauma-informed CBT for hypervigilance
- Safety planning maintained

**Outcome**: SPIA resolved with acoustic remediation; residual anxiety addressed through trauma therapy.

## 29.11 Conclusion: The Social Is Not Separate

SPIA cannot be understood in purely technical or purely psychiatric terms. The social context—of surveillance, abuse, harassment, and harm—is integral.

**Key principles**:
1. Surveillance is real and documented
2. Surveillance causes psychological harm that predisposes to SPIA
3. SPIA can develop even after surveillance ends
4. Assessment must be trauma-informed
5. Neither dismiss nor uncritically validate
6. Integrate technical, clinical, and social understanding

The social dimension reminds us that CCA is not an abstract theory—it affects real people in real situations of vulnerability.
