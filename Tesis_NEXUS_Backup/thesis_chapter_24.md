# Chapter 24: Scenarios A-D and the E0-E4 Evidence Ladder

## 24.1 Introduction: Classifying CCA Cases

Not all cases of voice-hearing in technological contexts are alike. Some involve genuine external signals, others are purely endogenous hallucinations, and many occupy an ambiguous middle ground. This chapter presents the **Scenario A-D Classification System** for categorizing cases based on the dominant causal mechanism, along with the **E0-E4 Evidence Ladder** for calibrating the confidence of our conclusions.

## 24.2 The Four Scenarios

### 24.2.1 Scenario A: Exogenous Signal Dominant

**Definition**: The auditory experience is primarily caused by a genuine external signal (speech or speech-like) reaching the listener through technological channels.

**Characteristics**:
- Verifiable source (another person, recording, broadcast)
- Signal-to-percept correspondence confirmed
- Multiple listeners can verify content
- Technological pathway documented

**Examples**:
- Cross-talk on phone line (hearing another conversation)
- Accidentally unmuted microphone captured by intercom
- Echo/feedback creating intelligible speech

**Evidence Requirements**:
- Source layer: Calibrated recording shows speech content
- Perception layer: Multiple listeners agree on content
- Interpretation layer: High inter-rater reliability (Оә > 0.7)

**Clinical Implication**: Not a hallucination. Technical problem, not psychiatric.

### 24.2.2 Scenario B: Environmental/Acoustic Artifact Dominant

**Definition**: The auditory experience is primarily caused by non-speech acoustic energy (environmental or technological artifacts) that triggers false pattern-matching in the listener.

**Characteristics**:
- Detectable acoustic energy, but non-speech
- Spectral/temporal structure has SPIA potential
- Listener perceives speech; others do not (or perceive different content)
- Experience modulates with signal quality

**This is Classic SPIA/CCA**

**Examples**:
- Comfort noise perceived as whispers
- PLC-generated artifacts heard as words
- HVAC modulation perceived as voices

**Evidence Requirements**:
- Source layer: Acoustic structure present, SPIA metrics elevated
- Perception layer: Listener detects more than others; vulnerable profile
- Interpretation layer: Low inter-rater agreement; content varies by listener

**Clinical Implication**: SPIA diagnosis. Signal remediation + psychoeducation indicated.

### 24.2.3 Scenario C: Neuroperceptive Dominance (Endogenous)

**Definition**: The auditory experience is primarily generated internally, with minimal or no external acoustic trigger.

**Characteristics**:
- No detectable external signal at defined sensitivity
- Experience persists across environments/devices
- Content is consistent and often elaborate
- Other psychiatric symptoms may be present

**Examples**:
- Auditory hallucinations in schizophrenia
- Musical Ear Syndrome without external trigger
- Grief-related hearing of deceased voice (no radio needed)

**Evidence Requirements**:
- Source layer: No correlate at high sensitivity
- Perception layer: Experience independent of signal manipulation
- Interpretation layer: High consistency despite no acoustic structure

**Clinical Implication**: Psychiatric evaluation indicated. SPIA unlikely but not impossible as comorbidity.

### 24.2.4 Scenario D: Mixed/Indeterminate

**Definition**: Multiple causal factors appear to contribute, or evidence is insufficient to clearly assign to A, B, or C.

**Characteristics**:
- Some external trigger present, but insufficient to explain full experience
- Technical and psychological factors both operative
- Evidence is conflicting or incomplete
- Case may evolve over time between scenarios

**Examples**:
- SPIA patient who develops persistent symptoms after signal remediation
- Psychotic patient whose hallucinations worsen with specific devices
- Unresolved case with insufficient recording data

**Evidence Requirements**:
- Mixed or contradictory findings across FPI layers
- Or insufficient data to meet E2+ criteria

**Clinical Implication**: Continued monitoring, expanded assessment, multimodal intervention.

## 24.4 The E0-E4 Evidence Ladder

### 24.4.1 Purpose

The E0-E4 system prevents overconfident claims by calibrating what can be asserted at each evidence level.

### 24.4.2 Level Definitions

#### E0: No Evidence
- **Definition**: No systematic assessment performed
- **What can be claimed**: Nothing beyond raw self-report
- **Typical situation**: Initial presentation before workup

#### E1: Anecdotal Evidence
- **Definition**: Self-report only, or uncontrolled observation
- **What can be claimed**: "Patient reports X" (no mechanism implied)
- **Typical situation**: Clinical interview, informal observation

#### E2: Correlational Evidence
- **Definition**: Objective measurement shows association, but no controlled comparison
- **What can be claimed**: "X is associated with Y"
- **Typical situation**: Recording shows artifacts concurrent with reported experience

#### E3: Convergent Evidence
- **Definition**: Multiple independent lines of evidence point to same conclusion
- **What can be claimed**: "Evidence supports X as likely explanation"
- **Typical situation**: Source analysis + perception assessment + environmental modulation all consistent with SPIA

#### E4: Causal-Verified Evidence
- **Definition**: Experimental manipulation produces predicted effect
- **What can be claimed**: "X causes Y" (with appropriate caveats)
- **Typical situation**: Signal remediation eliminates symptoms; reintroduction reproduces them (ABAB design)

### 24.4.3 Evidence Ladder Matrix

| Level | Data Source | Control | Claim Strength | Action |
|-------|-------------|---------|----------------|--------|
| E0 | None | None | Cannot claim | Initiate assessment |
| E1 | Self-report | None | "Reports X" | Objective measurement |
| E2 | Recording | None | "Associated with" | Controlled comparison |
| E3 | Multi-layer | Partial | "Likely" | Consider intervention |
| E4 | Experiment | Full | "Causes" | Confident intervention |

## 24.5 Rules of Assertion by Evidence Level

### 24.5.1 What Can Be Said at Each Level

| Level | Acceptable Assertions | Unacceptable Assertions |
|-------|----------------------|------------------------|
| E0 | "Insufficient data for conclusion" | Any mechanism claim |
| E1 | "Patient reports hearing voices during calls" | "Patient has SPIA" |
| E2 | "Recordings show high SPIA potential" | "Recordings cause the experience" |
| E3 | "Evidence is consistent with SPIA diagnosis" | "This is definitely SPIA" |
| E4 | "SPIA is confirmed: elimination reproduced" | "All voice-hearing is SPIA" |

### 24.5.2 The Strong Evidence Negative

A critical application of E-levels is the **strong evidence negative**:

"No evidence of X" requires:
- Explicit statement of sensitivity (detection limit)
- Appropriate method for detecting X was used
- Method was correctly applied

Without these, "no evidence" is merely E0 (no data), not E4 (confirmed absence).

## 24.6 Integrating Scenarios and Evidence Levels

### 24.6.1 Minimum Evidence for Scenario Assignment

| Scenario | Minimum E-Level | Required Evidence |
|----------|-----------------|-------------------|
| A (Exogenous) | E3 | Recording + multi-listener + source trace |
| B (SPIA) | E2 | Recording + SPIA metrics elevated |
| B (confirmed) | E4 | ABAB elimination/reproduction |
| C (Endogenous) | E3 | Strong negative (E4 source) + persistent symptoms |
| D (Mixed) | - | Conflicting or insufficient evidence |

### 24.6.2 Upgrading Evidence Level

| Current Level | To Upgrade | Needed Action |
|---------------|------------|---------------|
| E0 вҶ’ E1 | Conduct interview | Structured phenomenological assessment |
| E1 вҶ’ E2 | Add measurement | Calibrated recording, audiometry |
| E2 вҶ’ E3 | Add convergence | Multi-layer FPI, environmental modulation |
| E3 вҶ’ E4 | Add experiment | ABAB elimination/reproduction |

## 24.8 Case Examples

### 24.8.1 Example: Scenario A (Exogenous)

**Report**: "I hear another person's conversation on my phone"
**Source**: Recording shows actual cross-talk
**Perception**: Three listeners independently transcribe same content (Оә = 0.85)
**Interpretation**: All agree on content, no distress

**Classification**: Scenario A, E4
**Action**: Report to telecom provider, technical fix

### 24.8.2 Example: Scenario B (SPIA)

**Report**: "I hear my name whispered during calls"
**Source**: Recording shows CNG artifacts, SPIA metrics high
**Perception**: Patient detects patterns others don't; fatigued
**Interpretation**: Blind panel hears nothing (Оә = 0)

**Classification**: Scenario B, E3
**Action**: Tier 1-2 SPIA intervention

### 24.8.3 Example: Scenario C (Endogenous)

**Report**: "An entity speaks to me constantly, regardless of device"
**Source**: Multiple recordings show no speech-like content (E4 negative)
**Perception**: Symptoms persist in silence, faraday cage
**Interpretation**: Consistent elaborate content; other psychotic symptoms

**Classification**: Scenario C, E3
**Action**: Psychiatric evaluation and treatment

## 24.9 Limitations and Caveats

1. **Scenarios can evolve**: A patient may begin with SPIA (B) and develop elaboration (вҶ’C) or mixed (вҶ’D)
2. **Evidence levels are not fixed**: New data can upgrade or downgrade
3. **Comorbidity is common**: Scenario B does not exclude co-occurring psychiatric illness
4. **Sensitivity matters**: Strong negative requires defined detection limit

## 24.10 Conclusion: Principled Classification

The Scenario A-D system provides:
- Clear categories for case classification
- Defined criteria based on FPI assessment
- Matched interventions for each scenario

The E0-E4 system provides:
- Calibrated confidence in conclusions
- Prevention of overclaiming
- Clear pathway for evidence accumulation

Together, they operationalize the central commitment of this work: **rigorous, replicable, ethically grounded** assessment of the intersection between technology, perception, and meaning.
