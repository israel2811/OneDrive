# Chapter 32: Linguistics, Semantics, and Language Structure

## 32.1 Introduction: Language as Filter

The perception of speech in noise is not a pure acoustic phenomenon—it is shaped by linguistic expectations. This chapter examines how language structure, phonotactics, and semantic priors influence SPIA, providing both theoretical grounding and practical implications for assessment.

## 32.2 Speech Signals vs. Noise: Acoustic-Phonetic Features

### 32.2.1 What Makes Speech "Speech"?

| Feature | Speech | Noise | SPIA Detection |
|---------|--------|-------|----------------|
| Periodicity | Quasi-periodic (voiced) | Aperiodic | Formant detection |
| Modulation | 4-8 Hz AM (syllabic) | Variable | Modulation spectrum |
| Spectral envelope | Formant peaks | Flat or random | LPC analysis |
| Harmonic structure | Integer multiples of F0 | No harmonics | Pitch detection |
| Temporal structure | Segments, pauses | Continuous | VAD-like metrics |

### 32.2.2 The Ambiguity Zone

Speech and noise share overlapping features in degraded conditions:
- Broadband noise can contain formant-like peaks by chance
- Amplitude modulation in mechanical sources mimics prosody
- Human perception is biased toward speech interpretation

## 32.3 Phonotactics and Language-Specific Priors

### 32.3.1 What are Phonotactics?

Phonotactic rules specify which sound sequences are legal in a language:
- English allows onset clusters like /str-/ (street)
- Spanish does not allow word-initial /s/ + consonant without /e-/
- Japanese does not allow consonant clusters

### 32.3.2 Phonotactic Influence on SPIA

**Hypothesis P3**: SPIA content follows the phonotactic rules of the listener's native language.

**Prediction**:
- Spanish speaker hearing noise → perceives Spanish-legal sequences
- English speaker hearing same noise → perceives English-legal sequences
- Phonotactically illegal sequences rarely reported

**Implications**:
- SPIA content is generated by the listener, not the signal
- Same acoustic input → different percepts by language
- Transcription analysis reveals listener's L1

### 32.3.3 Testing Phonotactic Predictions

**Experimental Design**:
1. Present identical ambiguous audio to native speakers of different languages
2. Collect transcription attempts
3. Analyze for language-specific patterns:
   - Syllable structure (CV, CVC, CCCVCC)
   - Consonant clusters allowed
   - Vowel inventory used

## 32.4 Bilingualism as Diagnostic Tool

### 32.4.1 The Bilingual Advantage

Bilingual/multilingual listeners provide a natural experiment:
- If SPIA content is in L1 only → endogenous generation
- If SPIA content shifts with context → expectation-driven
- If SPIA content is in L2 only → unusual, investigate

### 32.4.2 Clinical Application

In CCA assessment:
- Document all languages spoken by subject
- Note language of reported SPIA content
- Compare to language of actual audio source (if identifiable)
- Mismatch suggests SPIA; match suggests possible real content

## 32.5 Prosody, Rhythm, and Segmentation

### 32.5.1 Prosodic Features

| Feature | Acoustic Correlate | Perception |
|---------|-------------------|------------|
| Intonation | F0 contour | Questions vs. statements |
| Stress | Duration, intensity, F0 | Word meaning in stress-timed languages |
| Rhythm | Syllable timing | Language identification |
| Chunking | Pause patterns | Phrase boundaries |

### 32.5.2 SPIA and Prosody

Prosodic features are particularly susceptible to SPIA:
- 4-8 Hz modulation perceived as syllabic rhythm
- F0 variation perceived as intonation
- Pause-like gaps perceived as phrase boundaries

**The brain "hears" prosody before it hears words.**

### 32.5.3 Prosodic Cues in Artifact Audio

VoIP artifacts often create prosody-like patterns:
- PLC introduces durational variations
- CNG creates "pause-like" segments
- VAD transitions mark "utterance boundaries"

These artifacts are interpreted as prosodic structure by the perceptual system.

## 32.6 Computational Models of Language

### 32.6.1 N-gram Models

N-gram models predict next word based on previous N-1 words:
- Unigram: P(word)
- Bigram: P(word | previous_word)
- Trigram: P(word | previous_2_words)

**Application to SPIA**:
- SPIA content should follow high-probability sequences
- Unusual word sequences suggest creative elaboration
- Compare SPIA transcripts to corpus statistics

### 32.6.2 Entropy and Information

Shannon entropy measures predictability:
- Low entropy = highly predictable = common phrases
- High entropy = unpredictable = unusual content

**SPIA prediction**: SPIA content should have LOW entropy (common phrases dominate priors).

### 32.6.3 Limits of "Content" Analysis

**Critical principle**: We can analyze linguistic patterns without claiming to know meaning.

- Structure analysis: Objective (phonotactics, n-grams)
- Meaning attribution: Subjective (requires listener interpretation)
- SPIA assessment uses structure, not meaning claims

## 32.7 ASR Hallucination as Analogy

### 32.7.1 How ASR Hallucinates

Automatic Speech Recognition systems can produce:
- **Insertions**: Words not in audio
- **Substitutions**: Wrong words
- **Confabulations**: Coherent but false sentences

**Mechanism**:
- Acoustic model produces probability distribution
- Language model favors high-probability sequences
- Low-confidence acoustic input + strong language prior → hallucination

### 32.7.2 ASR as Model for Human SPIA

Human perception works similarly:
- Acoustic input is ambiguous
- Linguistic priors fill gaps
- Result is coherent but may not match input

**The analogy is not perfect** (humans have more context), but it illustrates the mechanism.

### 32.7.3 Never Trust ASR as Evidence

**Rule**: ASR output is never primary evidence of audio content.

- ASR reflects model training, not ground truth
- Different ASR systems produce different outputs
- Human blind panels are the gold standard

## 32.8 Semantic Attribution and Theory of Mind

### 32.8.1 From Sound to Meaning

The path from acoustic input to meaning:
1. Acoustic features extracted
2. Phonemic categories assigned
3. Lexical items activated
4. Syntactic structure built
5. Semantic interpretation constructed
6. Pragmatic inferences drawn
7. **Agency attributed** (who said this?)

### 32.8.2 Agency Attribution in SPIA

SPIA content often has attributed agency:
- "It was my neighbor's voice"
- "The phone said my name"
- "They were talking about me"

These attributions are **interpretations**, not evidence of source.

### 32.8.3 The Self-Relevance Bias

SPIA content tends to be:
- Self-relevant (name, concerns)
- Threat-relevant (danger, surveillance)
- Emotionally charged

This pattern reflects **top-down priors**, not acoustic content.

## 32.9 Cross-Cultural and Cross-Linguistic Considerations

### 32.9.1 Cultural Variation in Voice-Hearing

| Culture | Common Interpretation | Implications |
|---------|----------------------|--------------|
| Western secular | Mental illness | Stigma, treatment-seeking |
| Indigenous/spiritual | Spirit communication | May not seek treatment |
| Religious | Divine/demonic | Religious interpretation |
| Collectivist | Ancestral voices | Social integration |

### 32.9.2 Linguistic Variation

Language-specific features affect SPIA:
- Tonal languages: Pitch patterns interpreted as lexical tones
- Agglutinative languages: Long "words" from short segments
- Sign language users: Visual SPIA?

## 32.10 Practical Implications

### 32.10.1 For Assessment

- Document subject's L1 and all languages
- Compare SPIA content language to audio source language
- Analyze phonotactic patterns in reported content
- Use bilingual discordance as diagnostic clue

### 32.10.2 For Intervention

- Psychoeducation in subject's L1
- Explain that content reflects their own linguistic priors
- Reduce stigma by normalizing the mechanism

## 32.11 Conclusion: Language Shapes Perception

SPIA is not a passive reception of acoustic content—it is an active construction shaped by:
- Phonotactic rules of the listener's language
- Prosodic expectations
- Semantic priors
- Self-relevance biases

Understanding the linguistic dimension of SPIA provides both theoretical insight and practical diagnostic tools.
